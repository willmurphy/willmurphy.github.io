New Research: It's Fragmented Phone Use — Not Screen Time — That Overloads the Mind

Research published this month from Aalto University upends conventional wisdom about smartphones and cognitive overload. The study found that total daily screen time is a poor predictor of information overload and mental strain; the real culprit is the pattern of use — picking up a phone for brief moments and putting it down repeatedly throughout the day. Heavy users who engage in long, sustained sessions reported less cognitive burden than lighter users who checked their phone dozens of times. The finding has significant implications for regulatory and design approaches to digital wellbeing: time limits may be less effective than friction-based interventions that discourage compulsive re-engagement without restricting overall access.
